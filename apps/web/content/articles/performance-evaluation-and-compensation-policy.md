# Performance Evaluation and Compensation Policy

## Purpose

Define how Albert School evaluates performance, sets compensation, and
manages promotions, so that decisions rest on sustained impact, scope,
ownership, market reality, and internal fairness — not on negotiation
ability, personal proximity, tenure, or subjective impression. The policy
is calibrated to Albert's current stage (a growing, founder-led
organisation of ~35 people across several countries): enough structure to
be fair and defensible, without unnecessary bureaucracy. Version 1.0.

## Scope

All permanent employees of Albert School, across countries. Country
differences apply through compensation coefficients, local market
conditions, legal requirements, and employment practices. Freelancers,
contractors, interns, apprentices, and temporary workers may fall under
separate rules unless leadership explicitly includes them.

## The policy

### Compensation philosophy

Albert aims to pay competitively within the **relevant talent market** for
high-ownership, high-slope people who can help build an exceptional
education company — not the highest payer in every function, but fair,
internally coherent, and strong enough to attract, retain, and reward top
talent in roles critical to the school. Relevant markets are defined by
role, level, country, and skill scarcity. The default benchmark is
comparable education, higher-education, scale-up, and mission-driven
business roles; adjacent markets (technology, consulting, finance) are used
only for scarce or strategically critical profiles, not as the default.
Albert does not benchmark against every possible alternative career path —
only the relevant market for the role, weighing function, level, country,
scarcity, and strategic importance.

Five principles:

1. **Pay for impact and scope.** Compensation reflects the responsibility,
   complexity, and impact of a role; people are rewarded for value created,
   scope owned, and execution quality.
2. **Reward ownership.** Value end-to-end responsibility, proactive
   problem-solving, and reduced need for supervision.
3. **Preserve internal fairness.** Two people with similar roles, levels,
   scope, performance, and market context should be paid coherently;
   differences must be explainable.
4. **Stay competitive with the relevant market**, especially where talent
   is scarce or strategically important.
5. **Avoid negotiation-driven inequality.** Pay should not depend on who
   negotiates hardest, is loudest, is closest to leadership, or raises
   concerns at the right moment. Exceptions may exist but must be
   documented.

Transparency is an objective, but it must be *earned* by the quality of the
underlying system. Publishing uncalibrated salary bands would create the
appearance of fairness without the substance.

### Job architecture — four levels, two paths

Progression does not require becoming a manager. Senior levels are reached
through either a **management path** (leverage scales through people, team
performance, and operating models) or an **individual-contributor (IC)
path** (leverage scales through systems, expertise, judgment, institutional
knowledge, and cross-functional influence). Both can be high-impact;
neither is superior.

**Level 1 — Contributor.** Executes defined work with guidance.

- Delivers assigned work reliably.
- Learns quickly.
- Applies feedback.
- Requires regular direction or prioritisation support.
- Builds functional competence.

> Core test: *Can this person deliver well with guidance?*

**Level 2 — Owner.** Trusted to manage a domain, project, or recurring
responsibility end-to-end.

- Owns a clear perimeter with autonomy.
- Identifies problems and proposes solutions.
- Delivers reliably without constant supervision.
- Coordinates with others when needed.
- Improves execution quality over time.

> Core test: *Can this person be trusted end-to-end on an important topic?*

**Level 3 — Lead.** Owns a significant function, system, or strategic
workstream and improves how the organisation operates, not only their own
work. Progresses via management or IC path. Expectations for both paths:

- Owns complex and cross-functional topics.
- Raises the operating standard of the organisation.
- Structures processes, tools, and ways of working.
- Influences others beyond direct authority.
- Makes others more effective.
- Improves the system, not just their own output.

*Management path examples:* leads a team; sets team priorities; develops
people; manages performance; builds repeatable team rituals and processes.
*IC path examples:* owns a critical domain without necessarily managing a
team; becomes a reference point for expertise, judgment, or execution;
solves difficult cross-functional problems; builds tools, processes,
methods, or knowledge that raise organisational performance.

> Core test: *Does this person improve the system, not just execute tasks?*

**Level 4 — Head / Principal.** Owns a major function, strategic area, or
critical organisational system; responsible for outcomes, operating model,
and long-term direction. Managerial or IC.

*Level 4 manager (may be a Head of function):* defines strategy for their
area; builds and manages a high-performing team; makes strong
prioritisation decisions; owns outcomes, not only activity; represents
Albert with maturity internally and externally; creates scalable systems
and management routines. *Level 4 IC (may be Principal, Senior Lead, or
equivalent):* owns a critical domain with institution-level impact; sets
standards used by others; solves ambiguous, high-stakes problems;
influences strategy without relying on hierarchical authority; creates
systems, methods, partnerships, or expertise that materially improve
Albert; acts as a reference point for judgment across the organisation.

> Core test: *Does this person build the team, system, standard, or
> strategic capability that allows Albert to scale?*

**Titles** should reflect level, scope, and path. A person should not need
to become a manager to grow in level, compensation, or recognition if their
highest contribution is expert ownership, strategic execution, or
system-building. Albert avoids title inflation and never uses a title as a
substitute for compensation, retention, recognition, or difficult feedback.
Legacy title exceptions may exist temporarily but are documented and
progressively corrected. Promotion to a higher level requires all three of:
sustained strong/exceptional performance at the current level; evidence the
person is already operating at the next level; and a real organisational
need for the higher scope. Excellent performance alone does not justify a
promotion absent organisational need, and seniority in an IC role requires
broader leverage (systems, expertise, judgment, cross-functional influence,
institution-level impact) — not merely being experienced or very good at
the current role.

### Salary bands and transparency

Internal salary bands guide compensation decisions, defined by role family,
level, country coefficient, market benchmarks, internal equity, and
strategic importance of the role. Bands are currently internal — used by
the CEO, COO, and leadership team members involved in compensation
decisions — and are not automatically published to all employees.

Albert's objective is greater transparency over time, but full publication
should happen only once bands are built, tested, and calibrated against
current roles, levels, countries, and market data. Publishing too early
risks incomplete ranges, false comparisons between non-comparable roles,
and historical inconsistencies being read as intentional decisions. The
**phased approach**:

1. Build salary bands by role family, level, and country.
2. Map every employee to a role, level, and relevant band.
3. Identify gaps, inconsistencies, and exceptions.
4. Progressively converge compensation toward the bands.
5. Document justified exceptions.
6. Publish more detailed band information once the system is reliable
   enough to be useful and fair.

Until then, Albert communicates the philosophy, evaluation process,
promotion criteria, and salary-decision principles without necessarily
publishing all detailed bands.

#### Transition: September 2026 cycle

The September 2026 cycle will run on this policy's philosophy, evaluation
criteria, and rating scale, but not yet on salary bands, which are still
being built as part of step 1 of the phased approach above.
Band-dependent mechanics — the merit matrix by band position, below-band
corrections, and above-band caps — apply from the 2027 cycle onward.
Compensation decisions in 2026 will be documented with the same rigour as
all future cycles and retroactively mapped to bands once they exist; any
resulting gaps will be treated under the below-band correction rule. The
January 2027 mid-year check-in is the milestone at which bands must be
ready to confirm that band-dependent mechanics can activate for the 2027
cycle.

### Country coefficients

Because Albert operates across countries, bands may be adjusted by country
coefficients reflecting differences in local labour markets, cost of
hiring, salary norms, employer costs, legal/social-contribution structures,
and competitiveness for the relevant talent pool. Coefficients are reviewed
periodically and applied with judgment, not mechanically. A simple
coefficient table is maintained for France, Spain, Italy, Switzerland, and
any other relevant market.

### Position within salary band

An employee's position — below band, low in band, around midpoint, high in
band, or above band — influences future increases.

- **Below band:** normally corrected at the next compensation cycle, unless
  the gap is material and needs earlier correction. A material gap may
  justify an exceptional off-cycle adjustment, especially where the person
  performs strongly and the gap creates retention or fairness risk.
- **Above band:** salary is never reduced, but the exception is documented.
  Future raises may be limited unless justified by strong/exceptional
  performance, increased scope, promotion, market movement, or internal
  fairness. Being above band creates no automatic right to further
  increases.

### Performance evaluation cycle

- **One annual performance review** in July or August; salary increases,
  bonuses, and promotion decisions become effective in **September** where
  applicable.
- **One mid-year check-in** around January or February (adjustable to the
  academic/business calendar) to clarify priorities, give feedback,
  identify blockers, and avoid surprises at the annual review.

### Performance rating scale (four points)

**4 — Exceptional** (should be rare). Materially exceeds expectations for
the level.

- Creates outsized impact.
- Expands scope responsibly.
- Raises the bar for others.
- Solves difficult problems with autonomy.
- Improves systems, quality, or team performance.
- Is already showing behaviours of the next level.

**3 — Strong** (a very positive rating). Fully meets the expectations of
the role and level.

- Owns their scope reliably.
- Delivers high-quality work.
- Makes good decisions.
- Collaborates well.
- Requires limited supervision.
- Creates trust.

**2 — Inconsistent.** Shows strengths but does not yet perform consistently
at the expected level. Requires clear feedback and an improvement plan.

- Delivery is uneven; quality varies.
- Ownership is partial.
- Prioritisation requires too much support.
- Collaboration or judgment creates friction.
- Some objectives are met, but important gaps remain.

**1 — Not at level.** Not meeting the expectations of the role or level.
Requires a formal improvement plan and may lead to role change or exit.

- Repeated missed expectations; low reliability.
- Insufficient autonomy; poor judgment.
- Significant quality issues; role mismatch.
- Behavioural or cultural issues.

### Evaluation criteria

All employees are evaluated on five shared dimensions plus role-specific
objectives.

**Impact — does the person move important outcomes?** Examples: improves
student outcomes; improves admissions quality; contributes to revenue or
financial sustainability; strengthens company partnerships; improves
academic quality; increases operational reliability; makes the team more
efficient; strengthens the Albert brand; delivers strategic projects.
Impact is not activity — the question is whether the work changed outcomes
that matter, not whether the person was busy.

**Ownership — does the person take responsibility end-to-end?** Examples:
anticipates problems rather than waiting for escalation; follows through
until resolved; does not wait passively for instructions; escalates
intelligently; decides at the right level; reduces workload for others
rather than creating it; takes responsibility for outcomes, not only tasks;
moves topics forward even when ownership is ambiguous. Ownership is not
doing everything alone — it is making sure the topic moves forward with
clarity, accountability, and judgment.

**Quality and rigor — is the work reliable, precise, and scalable?**
Examples: produces work that can be trusted; checks details; uses data and
evidence; builds repeatable processes; avoids avoidable mistakes; improves
standards over time; documents important processes; decides with sufficient
analytical depth; protects Albert's reputation and credibility. Quality
matters because Albert operates in a trust-based environment — students,
parents, companies, professors, and institutional partners expect
professionalism and reliability.

**Collaboration — does the person make others, teams, and systems more
effective?** Examples: takes ownership of topics that must move forward
rather than staying narrowly within formal scope; meets commitments and
deadlines toward other teams rather than creating bottlenecks; actively
shares information, context, and decisions rather than withholding them;
develops, documents, and elevates the team rather than concentrating
knowledge; defuses conflict and resolves ambiguity rather than fuelling
tension or politics; looks first at what can be simplified, automated,
delegated, or improved with AI and better processes before asking for more
headcount or budget; helps other teams succeed without losing accountability
for their own priorities. Collaboration is not avoiding disagreement — it is
disagreeing productively, sharing early, and reducing friction.

**Culture and judgment — does the person behave in a way that strengthens
Albert?** Examples: acts with integrity; represents the school well with
all stakeholders; makes mature trade-offs; protects the long-term
reputation of the school; shows ambition without arrogance; handles
sensitive situations with good judgment; communicates directly and
respectfully; prioritises the institution over personal politics; builds
trust internally and externally. Culture is not a personality type — it is
behaviour that makes Albert stronger, more demanding, more trustworthy, and
more effective.

**Role-specific objectives.** Each person is also assessed against
objectives specific to their role — e.g. admissions targets and quality;
student satisfaction and retention; company partnership outcomes; academic
delivery; campus operations; marketing performance; financial discipline;
hiring and team development; strategic project delivery. These should be
defined before the review period whenever possible.

**Additional criteria for managers** (four dimensions): **team
development** — do their people grow, perform, and receive clear feedback?;
**hiring bar** — do they attract, select, and retain strong people?;
**operating system** — do they build processes, rituals, and tools that make
the team more effective?; **management courage** — do they address
underperformance, conflict, and ambiguity directly? A manager is measured by
the quality, clarity, and effectiveness of the team they build, not only
personal output.

**Additional criteria for senior individual contributors:** the leverage
created beyond their own output — building tools, processes, methods, or
standards used by others; becoming a reference point for expertise or
judgment; solving complex cross-functional problems; raising quality across
the organisation; creating institutional knowledge; helping others decide
better; driving strategic outcomes without formal authority. A senior IC is
measured by the broader leverage they create, not only personal excellence.

### Review process

**Self-review** (completed before the annual review; an input, not a
determinant of the rating). Answers: (1) main contributions this year; (2)
outcomes most proud of; (3) where they fell short; (4) feedback received or
acted on; (5) priorities for next year; (6) support needed to perform at the
next level.

**Manager review.** The manager prepares an assessment using the shared
dimensions, role-specific objectives, evidence from the year, feedback from
relevant stakeholders, the self-review, and comparison with level
expectations. The manager proposes a performance rating, development
priorities, promotion readiness (if relevant), and a compensation
recommendation (if relevant). Assessments must be evidence-based; vague
impressions are not sufficient.

**Calibration.** Leadership calibration ensures fairness and consistency
across teams. The group includes the CEO, COO, relevant leadership team
members, and other managers where appropriate. Its purpose: compare ratings
across teams; challenge inflated or overly harsh ratings; check consistency
by level; identify internal equity issues; review compensation exceptions;
and reduce bias linked to visibility, personality, negotiation style,
gender, age, tenure, or proximity to leadership. Calibration asks: *compared
with others at the same level and similar scope, did this person create
more, equal, or less value?* Final compensation and promotion decisions are
made through calibration, with **final sign-off by CEO and COO**.

### Evaluation of leadership team members

Leadership team (LT) members are subject to the same cycle, evaluation
criteria, rating scale, and documentation requirements as all employees.
There is no carve-out for level or seniority.

Each LT member's review starts with a **self-review** (same six-question
format as all employees, submitted before the review meeting). It is then
prepared jointly by the CEO and COO and must include structured written
input from: (a) 1–2 LT peers, and (b) at least 2 members of the person's
own team, for LT members who manage direct reports. For senior ICs without
a team, team input is replaced by structured input from 1–2 close
cross-functional collaborators. "Structured" means a written assessment
against the same five shared dimensions (impact, ownership, quality and
rigour, collaboration, culture and judgment), submitted before the review
meeting. LT ratings, raises, bonuses, and any exceptions go through
calibration and appear in the same documentation record as everyone else's.

The CEO and COO review each other under the same mechanism: each prepares a
self-review first, then a written assessment of the other using the five
shared dimensions, with peer input from 1–2 LT members and team input from
at least 2 direct reports, submitted before the review meeting. If Albert
establishes a board or advisory body with governance responsibility, that
body takes over the review of the CEO and COO in place of the
mutual-review mechanism described here.

### Compensation decisions and merit matrix

Compensation is guided by a merit matrix but is not purely mechanical.
Increases may depend on performance rating, position within band, market
movement, scope increase, promotion, internal equity, and strategic
importance of the role. The following should **not** normally drive
compensation: personal financial need; loyalty alone; tenure alone; effort
without impact; negotiation pressure; threats to leave; personal closeness
to leadership. Retention risk may be considered but must not override
fairness or create unjustified exceptions.

Merit-matrix principles by rating:

- **Exceptional:** may receive a strong increase, especially if low or
  around midpoint in band; if already high in band, a more limited increase
  and a bonus may be more appropriate.
- **Strong:** a normal or solid increase depending on band position, market
  movement, and scope; smaller if already high in band.
- **Inconsistent:** usually no raise, unless correcting a major under-market
  or internal-equity issue.
- **Not at level:** no raise; the priority is performance improvement, role
  adjustment, or exit.

### Bonuses and variable compensation

**Bonuses** are limited and discretionary — not automatic. They may be used
when: a person delivered exceptional impact; made a contribution beyond
normal scope; solved a critical problem for the school; a raise is not
appropriate because the contribution was exceptional but not recurring; or a
high-in-band person deserves recognition for exceptional performance.
Bonuses are documented and calibrated.

**Variable compensation** may apply to roles such as admissions, sales,
corporate partnerships, or business development, but in an education company
it must be designed carefully: it must never reward volume at the expense of
quality, student fit, academic standards, or long-term reputation, and every
plan must include quality safeguards — e.g. student quality; retention;
conversion quality; company satisfaction; payment reliability; compliance
with admissions standards; no aggressive or misleading sales behaviour.
Variable plans must be approved by CEO and COO.

### Promotions

Normally considered at the annual review cycle; off-cycle promotions are
rare and must be justified by a real change in scope. Promotion requires all
three conditions: (1) sustained strong or exceptional performance; (2)
evidence the person is already operating at the next level; (3)
organisational need for the higher scope. Promotion is **not** automatic
from: tenure; loyalty; workload; personal ambition; retention risk; a strong
year at the current level; or a title promised informally in the past. A
promotion usually carries a raise, sized by the new level, new salary band,
current position in band, market context, and internal equity. Promotions
run through either the management path (increased responsibility for team
performance, people management, hiring, prioritisation, operating model, and
outcomes) or the IC path (increased responsibility for systems, expertise,
strategic execution, cross-functional influence, institutional knowledge, or
high-stakes problem-solving). Neither path is superior.

### Underperformance

Addressed directly and quickly; managers give clear feedback early, not at
the annual review. For an inconsistent or not-at-level rating the manager
defines: the performance gap; the expected standard; the actions required;
the support available; the timeline for improvement; and the consequences if
improvement does not happen. A not-at-level rating triggers a **formal
improvement plan**. Persistent underperformance may lead to role change,
level change, or exit.

### Documentation

All compensation decisions must be documented, including: current role and
level; current compensation; relevant salary band; performance rating;
raise or bonus decision; promotion decision (if any); rationale; and any
exception to policy. Exceptions must be explicit — e.g. above-band salary;
off-cycle raise; retention adjustment; market adjustment; promotion outside
the annual cycle; unusual bonus; title-level mismatch. LT member reviews —
including the CEO and COO mutual review — are documented in the same record
as all other employees. Appeal outcomes are also documented alongside the
original decision: the written request, the identity of the re-reviewer,
the decision, and its rationale. Documentation exists for fairness, memory,
and discipline — not bureaucracy.

### Communication to employees

Employees should understand how decisions are made. Albert communicates: the
review calendar; the performance dimensions; the rating scale; the promotion
principles; the general compensation philosophy; the fact that decisions
consider performance, scope, market, band position, and internal fairness;
and the fact that progression is possible through both management and IC
paths. Albert does not currently commit to publishing individual salaries or
full bands — detailed transparency is useful only once levels, bands,
country coefficients, and historical exceptions have been reviewed;
publishing prematurely could create confusion, false comparisons, or a sense
of precision the system has not yet earned.

### Appeals

Any employee may request a re-review of their performance rating or
compensation decision within 30 days of receiving the communication. The
request must be in writing and must state the specific disagreement —
rating, raise, band placement, or exception. The re-review is conducted by
someone other than the original decision-maker: if the manager made the
call, it is reviewed by the CEO or COO; if Grégoire made the call, Mathieu
answers — and vice versa. The answer is delivered in writing within 30 days
and documented alongside the original decision; the outcome can confirm,
adjust, or escalate to the next calibration cycle. One appeal per decision;
using the appeal path carries no penalty and must never be treated as a
negative signal in future reviews.

### Responsibilities

- **CEO and COO:** own the policy; ensure fairness across the organisation;
  approve salary bands; lead calibration; validate promotions, raises,
  bonuses, and exceptions; conduct the mutual CEO/COO review using the same
  peer- and team-input mechanism applied to all LT members; handle appeal
  requests escalated above the manager level; confirm at the January 2027
  check-in that salary bands are ready to activate band-dependent mechanics
  for the 2027 cycle; update the policy as the organisation grows.
- **Managers:** set clear expectations; give regular feedback; prepare
  evidence-based reviews; address underperformance; propose fair
  compensation decisions; avoid promises that have not been approved.
- **Employees:** own their performance; seek feedback; complete self-reviews
  honestly; raise concerns constructively; use the appeal path if they
  disagree with a decision; understand that compensation depends on impact,
  scope, market, and fairness — not only individual preference.

The policy is reviewed at least annually and evolves as Albert adds
countries, functions, or a dedicated People leader — simple, but not
informal. The objective is to preserve high standards, fairness, and trust
as Albert scales.

## Exceptions

Compensation exceptions are permitted but must be **explicit and
documented**: above-band salaries; off-cycle raises; retention and market
adjustments; off-cycle promotions; unusual bonuses; and title-level
mismatches. Adjacent-market benchmarking (technology, consulting, finance)
is itself an exception reserved for scarce or strategically critical
profiles. Freelancers, contractors, interns, apprentices, and temporary
workers are out of scope unless leadership explicitly includes them.
